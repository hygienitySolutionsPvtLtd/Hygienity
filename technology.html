Nanotechnology designed to make sanitation surfaces easier to maintain.

Hygienity’s coating approach is built around changing the way a surface behaves. Instead of treating hygiene as only a recurring cleaning problem, the company is developing a thin nano-enabled layer that may help reduce the adhesion of stains, residue, and moisture-related contaminants.

Simple Explanation

Think of the coating as a functional shield applied over hygiene infrastructure. It is being engineered to make surfaces less welcoming to persistent dirt, water marks, and microbial buildup, so that cleaning becomes easier and more consistent over time.

Anti-microbial intent

Supports healthier surfaces

The material system is being developed to support reduced bacterial growth patterns on coated areas, based on pilot observations. It is intended as a preventive support layer rather than a substitute for cleaning protocols.

Hydrophobic behavior

Encourages easier runoff

A key property is water repellency. By encouraging liquid runoff, the coating may help reduce stain formation and slow the buildup of visible residue in high-use washrooms.

Longer-term layer

Designed for durability

The formulation focus is on maintaining performance over operational cycles instead of requiring constant reapplication, while validation work continues in real environments.

Application Process

How the coating is intended to be used

01

Surface assessment

The substrate is reviewed for compatibility, current contamination patterns, and maintenance conditions.

02

Preparation

Surfaces are cleaned and prepared so the coating can form a stable, uniform treatment layer.

03

Coating application

The nano coating is applied using a controlled process suited to toilets, basins, or sanitation fixtures under evaluation.

04

Pilot monitoring

Performance is observed through cleaning cycles to understand durability, ease of maintenance, and user-visible outcomes.

Why It Matters

A material science response to an operations problem.

Sanitation infrastructure often degrades into a cycle of staining, odor, and repeated intensive cleaning. Hygienity is exploring whether engineered surface performance can interrupt that cycle earlier and more efficiently.

Important Note

Validation is ongoing.

All benefits described on this page are framed as development goals or pilot-based observations. Hygienity presents them conservatively while additional testing and field learning continue.
